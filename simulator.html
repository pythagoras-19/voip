VoIP Network Simulator

Experience real VoIP scenarios and understand how network conditions affect call quality

How to Use the Simulator

1. Adjust Network Parameters

Use the sliders and dropdown to configure different network conditions. Start with default settings and gradually increase network load and packet loss to see how they affect call quality.

2. Start the Simulation

Click "Start Call Simulation" to begin the VoIP call. Watch the visual representation of packets flowing between the phones and server.

3. Analyze Results

Review the call analysis metrics including MOS score, latency, jitter, and packet loss. Understand how each parameter affects overall call quality.

4. Experiment & Learn

Try different combinations of settings to understand real-world scenarios. Compare excellent vs poor network conditions to see the impact on VoIP performance.

Why Understanding VoIP Network Conditions Matters

Real-World Application: In actual VoIP deployments, network conditions directly impact user experience. This simulator helps you understand the relationship between network parameters and call quality.

Quality of Service (QoS): VoIP is sensitive to network impairments. Understanding how latency, jitter, and packet loss affect call quality is crucial for designing reliable VoIP systems.

Troubleshooting Skills: When VoIP calls have issues, you need to identify whether the problem is network-related or application-related. This simulator builds your diagnostic skills.

Network Planning: Before deploying VoIP, you must ensure your network can handle the traffic. This tool helps you understand the requirements and plan accordingly.

Key Metrics Explained:

  • MOS Score (1-5): Mean Opinion Score - subjective quality rating. 4.0+ is excellent, 3.0-3.9 is good, below 3.0 indicates poor quality.
  • Latency (ms): Round-trip delay. Under 150ms is acceptable, over 300ms causes noticeable delays.
  • Jitter (ms): Variation in packet arrival times. Under 30ms is good, over 50ms causes audio distortion.
  • Packet Loss (%): Percentage of packets that don't arrive. Under 1% is acceptable, over 2% significantly degrades quality.
